Chapter 01 · Getty Images / iStock

Generate now, license later: making AI reference images feel free without hiding the bill

Sole content designer on the working team · April 2024 – May 2025 · Shipped on two brands

The challenge

Getty Images' commercially safe AI generator needed reference images: pair a text prompt with an existing image and choose whether it influences the color or the composition of a new generation. The hardest phase let users reference stock images they hadn't licensed yet — on a platform whose entire business is licensing.

Research kept returning one theme: users didn't resent paying — they resented finding out late. "Bait-and-switch" paywalls were the top trust killer. So the brief became: let someone generate freely with an image they haven't paid for, make the eventual license requirement unmissable, and never once feel like a trap.

My role

Sole content designer with two product designers, a researcher, and a PO. I owned the copy system end to end — feature copy, education, error/empty/disabled states — plus launch QA, research distillation, upload legal and privacy language, and stakeholder walkthrough scripts.

Terminology systemsTrust & licensing UXResearch translationLaunch QA

Locking the vocabulary

The first job was naming the two influence options. I rejected "Influence priority" (names the mechanism, not the choice) and "Select what to take from your reference image" ("take" is too aggressive for a creative tool). We landed on Focus on color ("Transfer color palette and tone") and Focus on composition ("Transfer compositional elements like pose").

When review feedback showed users didn't realize the onboarding examples were new generations, I iterated the step heading until it taught the causality itself: "Select what to focus on and generate."

Disabled states that give the reason, not the shrug

With a reference active, camera controls and aspect ratios lock. The default copy said "Unavailable" — which implies broken. I set the rule: "disabled" (intentional, temporary), never "unavailable" (missing) — and always state why.

The licensing flow: clarity couldn't override truth

The unlicensed-reference flow had four beats to land, in order: a reference was used; you must license it to download; licensing supports the original creator and gets you both images; pricing is on the next screen.

The hardest decision was rejecting "Use without licensing" as a CTA — even though it tested as the clearest — because it implied users could proceed and assume the copyright risk themselves, which is legally wrong. I also banned "unlock" (paywall vocabulary), banned "free" anywhere (generating still costs credits), and rejected "Pre-shot images" as a tab label (ambiguous).

User testing showed people assumed limited functionality without upfront licensing, so the CTA pair evolved to "Generate now, license later" / "License now" with equal prominence — leading with full functionality, matching the trial-first mindset research kept surfacing.

Legal confirmed the exact moment of licensing didn't matter, only that the requirement was always clear — which freed the flow to stay friction-light.

Outcomes

  • Phase 1 shipped on both Getty Images and iStock in July 2024; the uploads phase went kickoff to dev-ready in six weeks; the unlicensed-reference direction locked in November 2024 and entered build in January 2025.
  • In usability testing of the reference-image experience: 86% comprehension, 72% felt in control of outcomes, 100% downloaded an image. One of eight features I landed across Getty & iStock.
  • Launch QA caught two cross-brand blockers before release.
  • The pattern set — reference mental models, license-later flows, restriction overlays — became the foundation of Getty's next-generation prompt-based editing product. Chapter 02 is its sequel.

"Clarity can't override truth. The clearest label in the test was still the wrong one — the craft is finding the phrasing that's both."What Chapter 01 taught me

Chapter 02 · Getty Images / iStock

A language system for AI editing that tells the truth

Content designer, prompt-based editing · 2025 – 2026 · The sequel to Chapter 01 — two years of continuity on one product family

The challenge

Getty's prompt-based editing product layered natural-language editing onto Getty and iStock: type "make it night" and the image changes. The catch lived in the model stack — the editing model output 1K natively, and the AI enhancement that produced the larger files stock customers expect could literally change the picture. Internal QA had flagged altered textures, skin, even a tooth gap. Usage data showed users gravitate to the largest download option — exactly the one most likely to change their image.

Three problems stacked: mental-model load (three editing spaces, set-level vs single-image edits); the truth problem (internal QA language — "deformities," "artifacts" — could never ship, but its substance had to); and the trust problem (we couldn't recommend one option over another, and in a stakeholder dry-run my own pros/cons slides were called out as quietly steering the room).

My role

Content designer owning the product's language across UI and design reviews: taxonomy, CTAs, system-response vocabulary, guidance and error copy, download expectation-setting, and the review decks themselves.

The taxonomy: three verbs that carry scope

The core deliverable was one sentence. Refine is additive and set-level; Modify is precise and single-image — the word pair signals "all images vs one image" without explanatory UI. From it came a locked CTA verb system and a placement rule: outside the editor, copy sets expectations; inside the editor, copy drives action.

System responses repeat dozens of times per session. Humanized copy ("Your changes are live!") reads as fake when stacked in a feed — so the grammar is deliberately robotic-safe, on purpose.

Download copy: from selling to describing

The download modal iterated from overpromise ("Upscaled without changing your image" — false) toward a neutral system. The breakthrough was reframing: these are different behaviors, not pros and cons. "May change small features" isn't a con, and the ✓/✕ icons were quietly telling users what to pick. I dropped the judgment framing for a "What to expect" expander — both options written at equal length so neither reads as the winner.

That "Details" line is internal QA truth translated into user language: never ship the lab notebook, never hide its conclusions.

The sandbagging fix

In a design-review dry-run, a design leader called out that one option's cons looked self-created — sandbagging. Rather than soften the deck, I diagnosed the mechanics: asymmetric framing, judgment words, quantity imbalance, softeners on one side only. Then I rewrote all five option slides symmetric and neutral, and built parallel framing sets so tone can swap without changing substance. The method was adopted into the team's design-review format.

Chapter 03 · Samsung Knox · this one runs dark, like the subject

The language of no: trust and guardrails for an enterprise AI assistant

UX content owner, AI assistant trust surface · Knox admin console · 2025 – 2026

The challenge

Samsung Knox is the enterprise security platform IT admins use to manage fleets of corporate devices. The team was putting an AI assistant into that admin console — a domain where a wrong answer can misconfigure security policy for thousands of phones, and where over-trust (acting without review) and under-trust (never adopting) are both failure modes.

Heading into the beta, the guardrail tickets had no user-facing messages — interventions would have surfaced as raw refusals. The live prototype's block message read "your request contains content that violates our policies," which convicts the user on every false positive. The entry screen stacked five disclaimers into legal wallpaper and promised a session-export feature that didn't exist.

My role

UX content owner for the assistant's trust surface: sole author of the guardrail messaging document (v1–v4), the naming strategy, and the measurement framework; negotiator of disclaimer and consent copy with legal, design, PM, and engineering; and owner of a 46-screen copy review that set house style rules.

Enterprise AISafety & refusal UXLegal negotiationMeasurement

Untangling what the system actually does

I started by asking why there were so many guardrail triggers — and traced every one to an engineering ticket, a stakeholder comment, or a section of the internal guardrail policy. The provenance table went into the messaging doc itself, so every message could be defended by pointing at its source.

The audit caught a real conflation: one "rate limit" message was doing three different jobs. I separated input limit, output limit, and runtime limit — each with recovery advice the user can actually follow. The old copy told admins to "wait and try again" — right for throttling, wrong for the other two. Untangling system truth before writing copy is the job.

Refusal moments are where trust is won or lost — and exactly where teams spend the least design time. Nobody demos the block message.

Interactive — Fix this message. The live prototype's block message, with its problems marked. Tap each one, then rewrite it.
"I'm sorry, your request contains content that violates our policies."

Tap a highlighted problem ↑

"Your message could not be processed because it may violate our content policy. Please rephrase your request and try again."

The "may" does the ethical work — it covers false positives without withholding the reason, and the recovery path is one the user can actually take. Constraints this sentence survived: one configurable message for all filter types, a security rule against revealing which guardrail fired, a stakeholder demand for a reason, and a user who deserves not to be accused.

Disclaimers, consent, and the legal negotiation

The entry screen stacked terms language, data-usage language, accuracy hedges, a translation warning, and an input warning. I lost the push to cut legally required lines — and won the fight that mattered: structure. One tightened paragraph up top, one persistent line under the input at the moment it matters. Pitching the change as structure-not-content is why legal said yes.

I rejected the translation warning's "use English for best results" — it reads as English-superiority and breaks when the user already is asking in English. A verb-level analysis landed: "[The assistant] supports English. Translated responses may be less accurate." I also caught a trust-breaking claim — a promised session-export feature that didn't exist — and replaced it with honest 30-day retention copy.

Naming and measurement

The renaming brief came with hard constraints (no "AI" in the name, must fit the product family). I rebuilt the strategy on naming research; three team favorites died on receipts, not taste: a trademark collision, a toxic association, a partner's product name. For measurement, I wrote the framework that answers "what's the KPI?" with there isn't one — four pillars, with guardrail metrics first-class (rollback frequency, bulk actions without review): success is not speed at the cost of control.

Outcomes

  • 13 guardrail messages finalized and mapped across five engineering tickets; 9 shipped for the beta, 2 consciously parked with tracking tickets.
  • Legal accepted the disclaimer restructure; the false export claim became honest 30-day retention copy.
  • A 46-screen copy review produced ~55 findings — including that all 46 screens had zero error, empty, or permission states.

"With legal, pitch structure, not content. That one sentence has paid for itself a dozen times."What Chapter 03 taught me

Further studies · 04 – 07

The supporting evidence

Four more, told shorter — each one ending with what it cost me to learn. Full versions available in conversation, where the good details live anyway.

Study 04 · Samsung Knox

Six thousand terms, seven products, one vocabulary

Seven enterprise consoles had drifted into seven dialects — "EMM" meant "UEM" meant "MDM," sometimes in the same product. I built the suite's first complete terminology inventory, then the governance to keep it honest.

  • Capture, don't fix — every term logged exactly as shipped, because mixing inventory with improvement produces neither.
  • An AI-assisted deduplication pipeline that failed twice (inflating to 7,242 rows, then over-collapsing to 222 clusters) before the version that classified variants instead of erasing them — with a human between the algorithm and every merge.
  • ~184 glossary entries into structured review; decision frameworks for the contested terms ("agent" over "client"; plan vs bundle vs edition; "a policy applies one or more settings to a device").
  • Proved the model on a bounded surface: the 86-tooltip audit, every item with a disposition and a ticket.

"AI clustering fails silently in both directions. The fix is the same either way: keep the variants visible, and put a human between the algorithm and the merge."

Study 05 · Samsung Knox

One rule, thirteen responses: a design system for an agentic AI

An assistant that acts — pulling logs, re-queuing firmware, drafting policy — can't let "here's my plan" be mistaken for "I already did it." I designed the response system: 13 classes, a classification router, seven reusable chassis, and a model-facing runtime contract.

  • The one rule: primary-action styling is reserved for the single action that changes system state. Everything else is a reply or a utility. Approval on a plan is a reply; the button appears only at the true commit.
  • A four-question router classifies any response: changes state? needs input? safe without approval? produces a deliverable?
  • The taxonomy rewritten as an engineering contract — required/forbidden fields per class, derivation over introspection — adoptable because it ceded the enforcement mechanism.
  • Ten operational safety concerns collapsed into a Denied class (neutral, never red, never apologizes, always carries a route), three Confirm tiers, and honest partial-completion states.

"Content design at its most leveraged isn't writing strings — it's defining the taxonomy that determines which strings can exist."

Study 06 · Autodesk

The chatbot wasn't the problem. The missing judgment system was.

Everyone could tell the internal AI support assistant was sometimes wrong; nobody could say how, how often, or in which ways. I wrote the evaluation framework — then ran the pilot audit and caught the auditor AI hallucinating its own findings.

  • A 15-point LLM-as-judge rubric across five dimensions, with fabricated information an automatic zero and a critical-failure flag for hallucinations and broken escalations.
  • A five-category failure taxonomy — because a hallucinated deadline, a tone complaint, and a dead-end fallback all need different fixes.
  • The pilot's best diagnosis: a 9/15 thread whose root cause wasn't hallucination but state management — the bot regenerated everything each turn instead of applying the user's edits.
  • The meta-lesson institutionalized: judge output is untrusted until human-calibrated (κ ≥ 0.7), and every judgment carries row-level provenance.

"Evaluation is content design. Deciding what a good fallback looks like is language work — expressed as a rubric instead of microcopy."

Study 07 · iStock

Renaming a product line without breaking trust

Two collections renamed, a benefit removed, grandfathering rules to explain — a trust minefield where one wrong phrase had real money consequences. I owned the customer-facing copy: 158 text nodes, one source of truth, no euphemisms.

  • Killed "or you switch plans" everywhere — it overstated the loss, and overstating a loss is as much a trust failure as hiding one. It would also have suppressed the exact upgrade behavior the project existed to encourage.
  • One sanctioned wording for the rollover rule, word-for-word identical across every FAQ, tile, tooltip, and modal that states it.
  • Caught an inverted rename mapping in stakeholder-approved copy — a one-word typo that would have reversed the entire rename.
  • Specified the conditional-messaging logic (plan tier × grandfathering status), because content design here meant writing the logic as well as the strings.

"Precision and kindness turned out to be the same edit — the softer drafts weren't gentler, they were wrong."

Field notes

Things I believe, learned the hard way

Every one of these came out of a real product decision — most of them out of getting it wrong first.

01

Clarity can't override truth.

The clearest label in testing was legally wrong. The craft is the phrasing that's both — and it always takes more rounds than either alone.

02

Disabled, never unavailable.

"Unavailable" implies broken. "Disabled" is intentional and temporary — and it always gets a reason. Users forgive limits; they don't forgive shrugs.

03

Say it once, in the right place.

If the overlay says it and the note says it, we don't need the tooltip saying it a third way. Redundancy isn't reinforcement; it's noise.

04

Never ship the lab notebook. Never hide its conclusions.

"Deformities" and "artifacts" can't reach users — but their substance must. Translating internal truth into user language is the whole job.

05

Robotic on purpose.

System responses that repeat fifty times a session are infrastructure, not conversation. Two words, noun + past participle, no synonyms — and no "Your changes are live!"

06

Honest process is a system property.

My "sandbagged" slides weren't dishonest in intent — they were asymmetric in mechanics. Fix the framework and the honesty follows.

About

The breadth is the point.

I'm a self-taught content designer from Vancouver who worked from Best Buy's product catalog to running content design for AI products at three major companies at once. No degree — just twelve years of treating every complex system as something that can be made legible, and building the system that makes it so. And the AI thread isn't new: I was writing Maya, Lemonade's insurance chatbot, back in 2018 — before "conversational AI" had a design discipline attached to it.

My instinct when facing chaos is to build a framework that makes the chaos navigable: glossaries, taxonomies, response systems, audit pipelines, measurement frameworks. I win through thoroughness, not flash — the signature work here isn't a beautiful screen, it's six thousand terms catalogued so that seven products finally speak one language.

And I treat feedback about my work the way I treat feedback about copy: as data. The sandbagging story in Chapter 02 is on this site because it started with someone telling me my slides were biased.

Working style — state a point of view, then open the door: "here's my recommendation and why — choose what fits."
Current arena — AI products, because probabilistic systems need honest language more than any interface ever has.
Also — I teach IA and content design at Vancouver Film School, run a small Etsy shop, and write songs. Ask about the aquarium.
